The Artist

Twenty Years of
Finding Her Voice

Katherine Houston didn't come to painting by the usual path. She spent the late 1970s through the 1980s in investment brokerage — a world built entirely on control, analysis, precision. Then she let go of all of it.

For over two decades, she has been building a practice grounded in the belief that the most powerful work happens when an artist stops fighting the medium and starts listening to it. She paints in reverse, working on the back of Plexiglas, so that every mark she makes is a commitment — no corrections, no layering over, no second chances.

"Control is the surprising outcome of letting go. When I stopped trying to dominate the surface, the paintings began to breathe."

Her training spans the Glassell School of Art, the University of Houston, and Hudson River Valley Art, with instruction under nationally recognized painters including Skip Lawrence, Quang Ho, and Arthur Turner. Her work now hangs in private homes, corporate offices, and galleries from Houston to Chicago to California's Carmel coast.

The Process

Painting in Reverse

Most painters build a picture from background to foreground — a logical, additive process. Katherine Houston does the opposite. She works on the back of transparent Plexiglas, beginning with the details that will appear in the painting's foreground.

The result is something impossible on canvas: a luminous, crystalline depth where light passes through layers of acrylic and refracts — generating an inner glow that seems to come from within the work itself.

Every mark is permanent. Every decision is final. The technique demands complete commitment and rewards it with paintings that collectors describe as unlike anything they've ever seen.

What makes a Houston painting unique
  • Applied to the reverse of transparent Plexiglas — not canvas, not board
  • Foreground painted first, background last — complete reversal of conventional practice
  • Light refracts through acrylic layers, creating inner luminosity
  • No corrections possible — pure intention in every stroke
  • Works appear to glow; impossible to replicate on other surfaces
